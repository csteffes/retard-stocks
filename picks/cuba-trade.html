Three Stocks to Trade American Intervention in Cuba

Tickers
$CCL , $SHERF , $SMIZF
Stocks covered
Carnival, Sherritt International, Meliá Hotels International
Publish Date
March 28, 2026
Hold Period
3-12 months

TL;DR

Look, I'm not saying you should bet your rent money on a penny stock mining company whose entire operation just got shut down by a US oil blockade, a Spanish hotel chain whose CEO is literally banned from entering America, and a cruise line that doesn't hedge its fuel costs while oil is at $100 a barrel. But maybe you should.

Cuba's grid has collapsed three times this month, Polymarket has Díaz-Canel at 64% odds of getting ousted by Christmas, and Trump just stood up at a Miami investor conference yesterday and said "Cuba's next" like he was ordering dessert. Castro couldn't kill these stocks, but maybe Trump will — or maybe he'll accidentally make them 10-baggers.

The thesis is simple: if the last communist domino in the Western Hemisphere falls, a $0.13 nickel miner restarts the only established mine on the island, a hotel chain with 35 empty Cuban properties suddenly has the most valuable beachfront real estate in the Caribbean, and a cruise line that already docked in Havana once gets to do it again at 20% ticket premiums. You're either early or you're wrong, and honestly it might be both.

Welcome to the Cuba trade.

Thesis

  • The market is treating Cuba as a humanitarian and diplomatic crisis first, but there is a real chance it becomes a capital markets repricing event second.
  • The best way to play that is not one stock. It is a spectrum: liquid tourism exposure through $CCL, maximum Cuba purity through $SHERF, and hard-asset hotel optionality through $SMIZF.
  • The common thread is simple: if Cuba moves from economic paralysis toward reopening, existing routes, mines, power assets, and hotel inventory get repriced much faster than the market is currently underwriting.

Why This Opportunity Exists

Most investors will never touch this setup because the inputs feel unserious until they don't. A prediction market spiking, a power grid collapsing, and a presidential threat aimed at Havana all sound like things to watch, not things to underwrite. That is exactly why the opportunity can exist. By the time it feels orderly enough for institutions, the cleanest asymmetry is usually gone.

The other reason this trade is interesting is that the market is still forcing everything into the wrong buckets. Carnival is being punished as an oil-sensitive cruise name. Sherritt is being priced like a distressed microcap on life support. Meliá is being treated like a diversified hotel operator with a messy side business in Cuba. All of that is true. None of it captures what happens if the island actually moves from sealed-off socialist relic to reopening trade.

Core Setup

One catalyst, three different payoff profiles:

  • $CCL is liquid and options-friendly
  • $SHERF is the purest Cuba proxy in public markets
  • $SMIZF already controls the hotel footprint people would rush to rebuild if they had the chance

The Crisis Backdrop

The report's case rests on the fact that Cuba is not dealing with a normal cyclical slump. The operating state of the country is visibly deteriorating. Oil imports that were running around 55,000 barrels per day last summer have effectively gone to zero. The grid has suffered seven major collapse events since February 2024, including three total nationwide blackouts in March 2026 alone. Online power generation has fallen to roughly 590MW against demand closer to 2,000MW.

At the same time, the political tape is getting louder. The report tracks Polymarket odds of Díaz-Canel being removed by year-end at 64%, with US strike odds around 34%. Whether you care about prediction markets or not, those numbers matter because they show how quickly a fringe macro scenario has turned into a tradeable public narrative.

The Charts That Matter

My Stock Picks

$CCL The Liquit Reopening Proxy

Carnival is the cleanest way to express a reopening in size. It is the only name here with deep listed options, and it already has real operational history in Cuba: the company sailed the first US cruise ship to Havana in over fifty years in May 2016. During the 2016 to 2019 window, Cuba itineraries reportedly carried roughly 20% ticket premiums. That matters because it means the Cuba angle is not a hypothetical product story. The route economics were already tested once.

The stock also comes with a useful contradiction. Q1 2026 delivered record revenue of $6.17B, record customer deposits of $7.9B, and a $2.5B buyback authorization. Yet the stock is down sharply because Carnival hedges none of its fuel costs and gets punished every time oil spikes. If oil normalizes and Cuba reopens in the same window, that double pressure can reverse quickly. The main risk is legal: the Havana Docks litigation could create real liability for cruise operators that used confiscated port assets.

$SHERF The Pure Cuba Lottery Ticket

Sherritt is the one that stops this from being a normal travel trade. It is effectively the purest listed Cuba proxy in public markets. The Moa nickel-cobalt mine and the Energas power assets are not side projects; they are the business. That is also why the stock is so violent. On February 17, 2026, mining at Moa was paused because the oil blockade choked off operations, and the associated Canadian refinery was set to run out of feedstock not long after.

At around $0.13 and a market cap near $65M, this is not a quality balance-sheet story. It is a distressed-asset optionality story. If Cuba stays shut and liquidity worsens, bankruptcy risk is real. The report flags C$316M of debt against only C$43.7M of Canadian liquidity, plus more than C$100M trapped inside Cuba. But if the island opens and Moa restarts, the stock no longer looks like a dying microcap. It looks like a dirt-cheap claim on an established nickel-cobalt operation and a critical-power asset inside the only place on earth that suddenly everyone would need to rebuild.

$SMIZF The Hard-Asset Hotel Footprint

Meliá is the most subtle version of the trade. Cuba is only about 2% of EBITDA, so this is not a pure-play event vehicle in the way Sherritt is. But Meliá matters because it already owns what other investors would need years to assemble: 35 hotels spread across Havana, Varadero, Cayo Coco, and other destinations. If Cuba opens, that footprint goes from politically trapped to strategically scarce almost overnight.

The underlying business is not broken. The report cites FY 2025 revenue of €2.08B and net profit up 24% to €200M, with the stock still trading well below its 2017 peak. What has changed is the Cuba-specific narrative. Several properties closed in February, occupancy cratered, and short interest surged. The CEO has also been barred from entering the US under Helms-Burton Title IV, which tells you this is not some apolitical hotel footnote. It is a geopolitical asset base wearing a hospitality label.

Variant Perception

The consensus version of this story is too shallow. It says Cuba is a humanitarian problem, maybe a diplomatic problem, and possibly a headline risk for a few fringe securities. My variant perception is that it is becoming an investable sequencing story. First comes the operating failure: energy, tourism, fuel, and logistics. Then comes the political narrative: blockade, sanctions, executive orders, and regime-change chatter. Then comes the repricing of who already owns something useful on the island.

That last step is where these three names matter. The market will not reprice every Cuba-adjacent security equally. It will pay first for liquidity, then for purity, then for asset scarcity. That is why the basket works better than a single-name obsession.

Legal Wildcard

The biggest non-obvious swing factor is Helms-Burton. The report highlights two Supreme Court cases argued in February 2026, including the Havana Docks dispute involving cruise lines. A broad ruling could increase legal pressure on anyone monetizing confiscated Cuban property. That is clearly a risk for Carnival and, to a lesser extent, Meliá. But it can also accelerate the broader political endgame by turning old confiscation claims into an active capital-markets overhang.

Catalysts & Timeline

  • The immediate catalyst is simply further state failure: zero oil imports, further power collapses, and visible deterioration in domestic operating conditions.
  • The legal catalyst is the Helms-Burton Supreme Court path, which could reshape how investors think about confiscated-property risk.
  • The macro catalyst is any Iran de-escalation or broader energy relief that changes the oil-pressure component of the story.
  • The political catalyst is either regime fracture, a negotiated transition, or a policy shift that lets tourism and investment reopen faster than current prices imply.
  • The market catalyst is simple recognition: investors figure out that these names are not random broken securities but claims on routes, rooms, mines, and power assets that suddenly matter again.

How I'm Trading It

The report's cleanest expression for a small account is not equal weight. It is role-based. Sherritt gets the biggest slice because it is the only true lottery ticket. Carnival gets the options capital because it is the only liquid name with listed leverage. Meliá gets the residual because it is the hard-asset ballast with less chance of total impairment.

What Would Change My Mind

  • If the crisis simply drags on without translating into either a political break or a genuine reopening path, the carry cost on the trade gets worse fast.
  • If the legal downside from Helms-Burton expands faster than the reopening upside for Carnival and Meliá, the thesis becomes much less attractive.
  • If Sherritt's capital structure deteriorates to the point where it cannot survive long enough to see the catalyst, the highest-upside piece of the basket stops being investable.

Key Risks

  • The biggest obvious risk is that nothing happens on a timetable the market can monetize.
  • Cuba can remain dysfunctional for longer than a trade can remain solvent, especially in the illiquid names.
  • Litigation, sanctions, or confiscation rulings can hurt the same securities that look like beneficiaries on first pass.
  • $SHERF in particular carries genuine bankruptcy risk; this is not a stylized downside, it is a live possibility.

Conclusion

This is not a normal stock screen. It is a geopolitical special situation wearing three different tickers. Carnival gives you the liquid version of the reopening trade. Sherritt gives you the distressed, high-beta version. Meliá gives you the asset-footprint version. If Cuba stays sealed and deteriorating, at least one of these names will probably get hurt worse before it gets better. If the island actually cracks open, they will not all reprice the same way, but they should all matter.
