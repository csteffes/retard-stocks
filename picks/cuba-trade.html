Three Stocks to Trade American Intervention in Cuba

This is the opening frame for a Cuba special-situation basket. The full memo is still in progress, but the setup is already clear enough to publish the names, the scenario, and the initial logic for why this could matter.

Tickers
$CCL , $SHERF , $SMIZF
Stocks covered

Publish Date

Hold Period

TL;DR

Look, I'm not saying you should bet your rent money on a penny stock mining company whose entire operation just got shut down by a US oil blockade, a Spanish hotel chain whose CEO is literally banned from entering America, and a cruise line that doesn't hedge its fuel costs while oil is at $100 a barrel. But maybe you should.

Cuba's grid has collapsed three times this month, Polymarket has Díaz-Canel at 64% odds of getting ousted by Christmas, and Trump just stood up at a Miami investor conference yesterday and said "Cuba's next" like he was ordering dessert. Castro couldn't kill these stocks, but maybe Trump will — or maybe he'll accidentally make them 10-baggers.

The thesis is simple: if the last communist domino in the Western Hemisphere falls, a $0.13 nickel miner restarts the only established mine on the island, a hotel chain with 35 empty Cuban properties suddenly has the most valuable beachfront real estate in the Caribbean, and a cruise line that already docked in Havana once gets to do it again at 20% ticket premiums. You're either early or you're wrong, and honestly it might be both.

Welcome to the Cuba trade.

Current setup

This first pass is a basket view, not a finished underwriting memo. The point for now is to establish the transmission mechanism. If US pressure on Cuba turns into a true regime-change trade, the market is unlikely to price that scenario evenly. It will probably hit the obvious tourism and Cuba-exposed names first, then move into the more obscure capital-markets beneficiaries once investors start mapping who actually owns stranded assets on the island.

That is why the basket starts with one liquid cruise proxy, one distressed Cuba-tied operating asset, and one hotel operator with direct room inventory already tied to the destination. The names are different. The common thread is that each gives you a different way to express the same geopolitical repricing.

Names in scope

  • CCL. Carnival is the liquid tourism expression: a straightforward public-market proxy for any reopening trade that brings Havana and Cuban cruise itineraries back into the book.
  • SHERF. Sherritt International is the most idiosyncratic name in the basket: a distressed nickel-and-power operator with direct Cuban exposure and far more operational leverage to a policy shift than its market capitalization suggests.
  • SMIZF. Meliá Hotels International is the real estate-and-rooms version of the trade: existing Cuban hotel inventory that could go from politically trapped to economically strategic very quickly in the right scenario.

What comes next

The deeper writeup will break this into three layers: the actual intervention pathways, the security-specific payoff profile for each name, and the main ways this trade can go wrong even if the political story keeps getting louder.

  • Path dependence: sanctions, blockade, negotiated transition, or outright regime fracture do not help each security in the same way.
  • Capital-structure reality: the most explosive names are usually the ones with the least room for error.
  • Timing risk: a correct geopolitical direction can still be a bad trade if the market gets there before the fundamentals do.

Status

This post is now live as the opening note for the Cuba basket. The full memo will follow once the rest of the operating and political context is added.
